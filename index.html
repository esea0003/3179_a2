Life Expectancy Around the World

A visualisation by Edelyn Seah

As the years increase over the 2000's to the 2010's, the progression of modern technologies, immunisation techniquess, healthcare systems and knowledge has significantly improved life expectancy rates. The charts below aim to demonstrate how immunisation metrics such as Hepatitis B, Polio, and Diphtheria, mortality rates in adults and children, economic factors, sociocultural influences, and healthcare indicators affect cross-country life expectancy rates. This analysis can serve as a valuable resource for nations seeking to refine their public health strategies and prioritise areas of intervention to enhance the well-being of their populations.

Infant Deaths Across all Countries in 2015

Infant mortality rates are typically due to lower immunity against diseases like Hepatitis B, Polio, and Diphtheria. Immunisation helps protect infants from life-threatening diseases, which, in turn, can significantly boost life expectancy rates.

This shows the infant mortality rates across all countries. We see that India has the highest number mortality rates of 1800 deaths in 2015. India may experience high infant mortality rates due to factors such as inadequate access to healthcare, limited prenatal care, and a higher prevalence of poverty, malnutrition, and preventable diseases.

Highest Mortality Rates

High adult mortality rates indicate a population's susceptibility to premature death during adulthood, often due to factors like chronic diseases, limited healthcare access, lifestyle choices, and socioeconomic disparities.

Based on the chart, we can observe that Lesotho has the highest adult mortality rates of 484 deaths per year by 2015. It is also important to note that in 2010, Lesotho is still ranked 2nd. Similarly to India in the previous visualisation, challenges such as malnutrition, high rates of poverty and lack of knowledge of vaccines could be the cause of high adult mortality rates.

Life Expectancy Rates in 2005 vs 2015

Between 2005 and 2015, the life expectancy rates might vary, reflecting improved healthcare, disease control, and overall public health. This incline signifies significant progress in enhancing population well-being.

Developed countries like Singapore, Australia, Germany, and Luxembourg tend to have higher life expectancy from 78 - 84 compared to developing nations such as India, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Uganda, and Nigeria (49 - 68). This difference is mainly due to better healthcare systems, higher living standards, and more effective public health efforts in developed countries. Developing nations face challenges like limited healthcare access and higher disease burdens, which contribute to lower life expectancy.

Diphtheria and Polio Immunisation Rates

Immunisation against Diphtheria and Polio diseases helps create herd immunity, making it difficult for the diseases to spread within the population. High immunisation coverage is crucial in minimising preventable deaths from these diseases, especially amongst vulnerable groups like children. The chart below displays the percentage of immunisation of these diseases among among 1-year-olds.

Developed countries typically exhibit high immunisation rates of above 90 consistently throughout the years, thanks to well-established healthcare systems, robust public health programs, and better access to vaccines. In contrast, developing countries may face challenges in achieving high immunisation rates due to limited resources, inadequate healthcare infrastructure, and barriers to vaccine distribution.

Years of Schooling on Life Expectancy Rates

Education equips individuals with more knowledge and skills to make informed decisions about their health, access healthcare services, and lead healthier lifestyles. It also fosters economic opportunities, which can improve living conditions and access to better healthcare. The chart below displays the possible correlation between number of schooling years and the life expectancy rates.

As shown above, populations with higher average levels of education like Australia (20.4) and Slovania (17.3), tend to have an average of 82-88 life expectancy in years, much higher compared to developing countries with lesser years of schooling like Slovenia and South Sudan, having 4.9-9.5 years, only to have low average life expectancies of 51-57. This is likely because developed countries tend to have a greater awareness of health practices , and also have better utilisation of existing healthcare systems for improved life expectancy rates.

Conclusion

In summary, factors such as immunisation, healthcare access, and education significantly influence life expectancy. Developed countries tend to have higher immunisation rates and healthcare access, contributing to longer lifespans, while developing nations face greater challenges in these areas. Increasing life expectancy over time underscores the potential for improving overall quality of life through better healthcare, access to education, and public health initiatives.
