Life Expectancy Around the World

A visualisation by Edelyn Seah

This study endeavors to bridge existing research gaps by utilizing a mixed-effects regression model, drawing upon data spanning from 2000 to 2015. It incorporates essential immunization metrics, including Hepatitis B, Polio, and Diphtheria, and systematically examines a range of variables, such as mortality rates, economic factors, sociocultural influences, and healthcare indicators. By conducting a cross-country analysis, our research aims to pinpoint the precise determinants contributing to decreased life expectancy. This analysis can serve as a valuable resource for nations seeking to refine their public health strategies and prioritize areas of intervention to enhance the well-being of their populations.

Infant Deaths Across all Countries in 2015

High infant mortality rates are a key contributor to lower overall life expectancy. The following graphs will highlight the role of immunization, specifically against diseases like Hepatitis B, Polio, and Diphtheria, in reducing infant deaths. Immunization helps protect infants from life-threatening diseases, which, in turn, can significantly boost life expectancy by reducing the burden of infant mortality.

This shows the infant mortality rates across all countries. We see that India has the highest number mortality rates of 1800 deaths in 2015. India may experience high infant mortality rates due to factors such as inadequate access to healthcare, limited prenatal care, and a higher prevalence of poverty, malnutrition, and preventable diseases.

Who is Immigrating? 👨👩

We can observe the proportion of each gender overall in the donut chart (right), or based on each age group in the population pyramid (left). Toggle the continent selection drop down to analyse the proportion for specific continents!
From the charts, the main observation is that most migrants fall in the age group of 30-49 years old. This can be explained by the fact that adults in this age range are more financially able to migrate to another country to seek a new life.

This shows the ranking of countries by the number of migrants in each 5-year period. We see that United States of America (USA) has the highest number of migrants across all periods.

Migration and GDP 🏦

From the chart, there is a clear positive correlation between a country's GDP and the number of migrants. This could be because a higher GDP would mean that the country is growing - which is an important factor when choosing where to migrate to. Clear examples of this are USA and China, as they have a high GDP and high number of immigrants (in 2020).

This shows the ranking of countries by the number of migrants in each 5-year period. We see that United States of America (USA) has the highest number of migrants across all periods.

Flow of Migration 🌊

The flow of migration highlights which continents migrants from one continent are migrating to. In this chart, we can analyse the flow of migration overall from each origin continent (left) to the destination continent (right).
One clear example from the chart is that the majority of migrants are from Africa, and a majority of these Africans are migrating to countries in Oceania.
We can also drill down on this chart by selecting on a continent's section of the bar on either side ("Origin" or "Destination") to see the proportions for only a specific continent to/from other continents.

Conclusion

Today, we can see that more people than ever live and become citizens in countries other than the one in which they were born in. Many individuals migrate either out of choice, or out of necessity. Therefore, from this visualisation we are able to inspect which countries and continents these immigrants are migrating to, as well as analyse the flow of migration trends. We have observed that there are many migrations that happen on a yearly basis, and therefore with so many migrations there is definitely a need to promote international cooperation on migration issues (complications, migrant deaths, displaced migrants).
